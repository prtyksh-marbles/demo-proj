## 3. The study, explained simply

> Marbles Health partnered with AIIMS Delhi — one of India's leading medical institutions — to clinically test the EASE protocol in people diagnosed with depression.

**Who was in the study:** 20 participants diagnosed with depression, ranging from moderate to severe.

**What they did:** Each participant went through 20 sessions over 10 days (two sessions a day). Each session had three simple parts:

1. A short brain-activity reading (EEG) before treatment
2. 20 minutes of gentle brain stimulation (tDCS) paired with guided cognitive-emotional exercises on a screen
3. A short brain-activity reading afterward, to track how the brain responded

**How progress was measured:** Depression severity was scored using the MADRS scale (a globally recognized clinical depression assessment) at three points — Day 0 (baseline), Day 5 (midpoint), and Day 10 (end of treatment).

**Suggested plain-language callout box:**
> Think of it like an intensive 10-day program rather than a once-a-week routine — the idea is to give the brain a concentrated, structured "reset" rather than spreading treatment out over weeks or months.

## 5. What "remission" means for patients

**Definition, in plain words:** Clinical remission means a person's symptoms have dropped low enough that they'd no longer be classified as having depression by the same scale used to diagnose them.

**Callout stat:**
> 12 out of 20 participants (60%) reached clinical remission by the end of the 10-day program. Two of them scored a perfect 0 — no detectable depressive symptoms at all.

Suggested framing sentence for the site:
> Remission isn't the same as "feeling a bit better" — it's the clinical marker doctors use to say someone's depression is, for now, gone.

## 6. How EASE works (simplified mechanism section)

Good for an illustrated/animated explainer section — this is inherently visual (brain regions, networks).

**Plain-language version:**

Depression is linked to imbalances in a part of the brain called the **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** — a region that connects three brain networks:

- **The focus network** — controls attention, decision-making, and follow-through. When it's off-balance, it shows up as overthinking or difficulty making decisions.
- **The self-reflection network** — active when we're introspecting. Overactivity here fuels rumination and negative self-talk.
- **The emotion network** — regulates how we feel and respond emotionally. Imbalance here shows up as mood swings, low motivation, or emotional flatness.

**How EASE targets this:**
- Gentle electrical stimulation (tDCS) is applied to the DLPFC to help rebalance activity across these networks.
- At the same time, guided cognitive-emotional exercises train the brain to reinforce that rebalancing.
- EEG readings before and after each session let the system "listen" to how the brain is responding — this is also the foundation for future personalization (adjusting treatment to each person's brain patterns).

**One-line summary for a hero/explainer:**
> EASE pairs gentle brain stimulation with guided mental exercises to help rebalance the brain networks most affected by depression — and reads brain activity along the way to track how well it's working.

## 7. Safety & regulatory standing

This section builds trust — patients want to know it's not experimental guesswork.

- The stimulation technology (tDCS) has been used in **over 33,000 documented sessions**, including **16,000+ specifically for depression**, across more than 1,200 participants — an established safety track record.
- The energy delivered during a session is roughly **1/20th of what your brain absorbs during a 20-minute phone call.**
- tDCS is approved for **self-administered home use in the EU and UK** (since 2019).
- In India, the **CDSCO (Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation)** classified tDCS as a low-to-medium risk medical device for depression treatment in **September 2020.**
- Similar regulatory recognition exists in **Singapore, South Korea**, and other countries.

**Suggested trust-badge copy:**
> Backed by a well-established safety record — not a new or unproven technology.

*(Note: the CDSCO classification above is for tDCS as a device category, not a claim that EASE itself has received a separate depression-specific approval — see caution note at the bottom before publishing this as a badge.)*

## 11. Suggested FAQ entries

**Is this a real clinical study?**
Yes — it was conducted at AIIMS Delhi, one of India's premier medical institutions, using a standardized clinical depression scale (MADRS) and structured assessment protocol.

**How long does treatment take?**
20 sessions over 10 days — two sessions per day, about 25 minutes each.

**Does it hurt / is it safe?**
The stimulation is gentle — about 1/20th the energy your brain absorbs during a 20-minute phone call — and uses technology with a well-established safety record (33,000+ documented sessions).

**Does everyone respond the same way?**
No. Most participants (17 of 20) improved substantially; a few had a slower or different course. Individual results can vary.

**Is this instead of medication or alongside it?**
The study evaluated EASE on its own; positioning versus/alongside medication should be worded per your medical/regulatory guidance rather than implied here.

## ⚠️ One thing to flag before this goes live

A few things worth a quick pass by whoever handles medical/regulatory review at Marbles, since this is patient-facing health content:

1. **The comparisons to antidepressants (2x faster, 2x remission rate, etc.) are cross-study, not head-to-head.** The study itself pulls those benchmark numbers from separate published research (Rush et al., Trivedi et al., etc.), not a direct trial against a medication arm. I've softened the comparison table language above, but "twice as effective as SSRIs"-style claims are the kind of thing that can trigger regulatory/advertising scrutiny for a medical device site — worth confirming exact phrasing with whoever owns regulatory compliance.
2. **n=20, open-label, no control group.** The study itself calls this a limitation. I've built in an honesty section (Section 9) that turns this into a strength ("early results promising enough that a 150-person controlled trial is already underway") — recommend keeping something like it rather than presenting the 63.8%/60% numbers with zero caveat, both for credibility and because overstated device claims are a real regulatory risk in this category.
3. **CDSCO classification** is for tDCS as a device category (Sept 2020), not necessarily a claim that EASE itself is separately CDSCO-cleared for depression — worth phrasing precisely rather than implying direct product approval, unless Marbles has that separately confirmed.

None of this blocks you from using the content above — just flagging so the copy that ships is defensible if a patient or regulator asks "where does that number come from."
